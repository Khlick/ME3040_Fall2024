Probabilty (Definitions)

Probability

The measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. Probability is quantified as a number between \(0\) and \(1\), where \(0\) indicates impossibility and \(1\) indicates certainty.

Sample Space (\(S\))

The set of all possible outcomes of a probabilistic experiment. For example, in a quality test of a car engine, the sample space might include outcomes like 'Pass' and 'Fail'.

Event

A subset of the sample space (\(S\)) that consists of one or more outcomes. An event can be something like 'Engine Fails' in a quality test.

Classical Probability

A probability approach where all outcomes in the sample space are assumed to be equally likely. The probability of an event \(A\) is calculated as: \[ P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of outcomes in event}}{\text{Number of outcomes in sample space}} \]

Empirical Probability

Probability based on observed data or past experiences, calculated by dividing the number of observed occurrences by the total number of observations.

Set Theory

A branch of mathematics focused on the study of sets, collections of distinct objects. Set theory underpins probability by defining events as sets and exploring relationships through operations like union, intersection, and complement.

Combinatorics (General)

Combinatorics deals with counting, arranging, and grouping objects. It forms the basis for calculating probabilities of events. Example: Counting the number of ways to arrange 3 objects: $3! = 6$.

Permutations

Permutations consider the order of arrangement. For $n$ objects arranged in $r$ positions, permutations are calculated as $P(n, r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$. Example: Arranging 3 out of 5 items: $P(5, 3) = \frac{5!}{(5-3)!} = 60$.

N Choose K Combinations

Combinations focus on selection without regard to order. The formula is $C(n, k) = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$. Example: Choosing 2 items from 4: $C(4, 2) = \frac{4!}{2!(4-2)!} = 6$.

Probability of a Simple Event

The probability of a simple event is the ratio of favorable outcomes to the total outcomes, $P(A) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total outcomes}}$. Example: Probability of rolling a 3 on a die: $P(3) = \frac{1}{6}$.

Probability of Union of Two Events

The probability of the union of two events, $A$ or $B$, is $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$. Example: Rolling a die, probability of rolling a 1 or a 2: $P(1 \cup 2) = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{3}$.

Probability of Intersection of Two Independent Events

For two independent events, the probability of both occurring is $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \times P(B)$. Example: Flipping a coin and rolling a die, probability of heads and rolling a 4: $P(H \cap 4) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{12}$.

Conditional Probability of Two Dependent Events

The conditional probability of event $B$ given event $A$ is $P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)}$, assuming $P(A) > 0$. Example: Drawing 2 aces from a deck without replacement: $P(A_2 \mid A_1) = \frac{3}{51}$.

Random Variables

A random variable $X$ is a function that maps outcomes from a sample space $\Omega$ to real numbers.

Mathematically, we write this as:

$$X: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$

There are two types:

Discrete Random Variables

Discrete random variables measure outcomes at distinct or finite points.

Examples include the count of defects in a product or errors in data transmission.

Continuous Random Variables

Continuous random variables can assume any value within a range of real numbers.

Examples include temperature readings, electrical current, or elapsed time.

Understanding random variables is crucial for analyzing data and making predictions in various fields.

In probability theory, a random variable is a fundamental concept that helps us quantify uncertain outcomes. A random variable is essentially a function that assigns numerical values to each outcome in a sample space. This allows us to work mathematically with probabilities and outcomes, converting qualitative events into quantitative analysis.

Random Variables

The expectation (or expected value) of a random variable is a measure of the central tendency of its distribution.

For a random variable \( X \) with discrete outcomes \( x_i \) and corresponding probabilities \( P(X = x_i) \), the expectation is given by:

$$E[X] = \sum_{i=1} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$

Consider a random variable with 2 equal bins: Bin $1\rightarrow 0$, Bin $2 \rightarrow 1$.

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 0 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \)

Now consider a scenario with 7 bins: mapped to 0 through 6 values

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 4 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 5 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 6 \cdot \frac{1}{7} \)

Simplifying:

\( E[X] = \frac{1}{7} (0 + 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) = \frac{1}{7} \cdot 21 = 3 \)

How would the expectation change if the bins were unequal sizes

Expectation is defined as the weighted sum of all possible values, where the weights are the probabilities of those values occurring.

To illustrate, let's consider splitting our sample space into bins and calculating the expectation as we increase the number of bins.

Discrete Example: Coin Flips

How many times must I flip a "fair" coin to get $P(H)=0.5$

As the number of trials in an experiment increases, the average of the results gets closer to the expected value (recall LLN). For a "fair" coin, this means that with enough flips, the proportion of heads ($P(H)$) approaches 0.5.

Assumptions:

  • Each flip is independent of previous flips.
  • The probability of heads, P(H), is constant, or identical at 0.5 for each flip.
  • The coin is "fair" $\therefore P(H) = P(T) \Rightarrow 1=P(H \cup T)=2P(H)\, \text{or}\, P(H) = \frac{1}{2}$

The Strong Law of Large Numbers ensures that $E[P(X=H)]=\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i = \mu $.

In the case of the "fair" coin, $\mu = 0.5$.

That is the expected probability is $E[P(H)] = 0.5$ for a $H$ in each flip.

So, what's the problem?

We need to understand the assumptions we make when asking our question, or positing our hypothesis.

There are two problems here:

  1. The question is aimed at the fairness of the coin, that is as the number of trials approaches infinity, the empirical probability will converge on the theoretical probability. This is impractical in experimental design, your CEO or finance department will laugh in your face if you ask for, near infinity samples.
  2. Lack of outcome specificity. Without a clear goal, or specified number of outcomes (say of heads), the question is too vague for practical experimentation.

Scenario: a coin is flipped 28 times, resulting in 19 heads.

If the coin is "fair", how likely is this

  • $P(X=k)$: probability of exactly $k$ successes in $n$ trials.
  • $\binom{n}{k}$: the number of ways to choose $k$ successes out of $n$ trials. $\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$
  • $p$: probability of success in a trial.
  • $1-p$: probability of failure in a trial (compliment).
  • $n$: total number of trials.
  • $k$: number of successes tested.

Using the binomial distribution formula:

$P(X = 19) = \binom{28}{19} (0.5)^{19} (0.5)^{9}=\class{info}{0.0257}$

Is this evidence of an "unfair" coin

$$P(X\geq 19) =P(X=19) + P(X=20) + \ldots + P(X=28)$$ $$= \sum_{x=19}^{28} \binom{28}{x} p^x (1-p)^{28-x}$$ $$= \class{danger}{0.044}$$
