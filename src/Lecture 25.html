Section Notes

Importance of Uncertainty Quantification in Engineering

Uncertainty quantification is crucial in engineering for ensuring the reliability and accuracy of measurements and designs. It allows engineers to:

  • Assess the quality of measurement tools and methods.
  • Make informed decisions based on the precision and accuracy of data.
  • Enhance the design and optimization of engineering systems.

Highlight the critical role of uncertainty analysis in all aspects of engineering, emphasizing its impact on decision-making and system design.

Overview of Systemic and Random Uncertainties

Understanding the different types of uncertainties is fundamental in their effective management:

  • Systemic Uncertainties: Often predictable and typically constant or systematic variations, which can be corrected or compensated.
  • Random Uncertainties: Arise from unpredictable variations during measurement and are generally treated statistically.

All uncertainty levels should be calculated to the same confidence level.

We typically would use 95% Confidence Intervals.

This slide introduces the two primary categories of measurement uncertainty, setting the stage for deeper exploration in subsequent slides.

Systemic uncertainties are also called Bias uncertainty.

Random uncertainties are also known as noise, or precision uncertainties.

Uncertainty Propagation Techniques Definitions

Maximum Possible Uncertainty:

Assumes that all errors contribute positively to the uncertainty, summing up linearly. Used for worst-case scenario analysis.

Mathematically, for variables \( x_1, x_2, ..., x_n \) with uncertainties \( \Delta x_1, \Delta x_2, ..., \Delta x_n \), it's calculated as:

\[ \Delta Y_{\text{max}} = \sum |\frac{\partial Y}{\partial x_i} \Delta x_i| \]

Root of Sum of Squares (RSS):

Considers the statistical nature of errors, assuming they are independent and random. Provides a statistically probable estimate of combined uncertainty.

Mathematically, it is:

\[ \Delta Y_{\text{RSS}} = \sqrt{\sum \left(\frac{\partial Y}{\partial x_i} \Delta x_i\right)^2} \]

Note: The partial derivative, $\frac{\partial Y}{\partial x_i}$, is called the sensitivity coefficient of variable $Y$ w.r.t. $x_i$.

This slide defines the two primary techniques for propagating uncertainties, setting the theoretical foundation for their application in engineering calculations.

Calculating Uncertainty: Ohm's Law Example

Using Ohm's Law \( V = IR \), where \( R = \frac{V}{I} \):

  • Voltage \( V = 100 \pm 5 \) V
  • Current \( I = 10 \pm 0.5 \) A
  • Sensitivity Coefficient for \( V \): \( \frac{\partial R}{\partial V} = \frac{1}{I} \)
  • Sensitivity Coefficient for \( I \): \( \frac{\partial R}{\partial I} = -\frac{V}{I^2} \)

Calculating maximum possible and RSS uncertainties:

  • Max Uncertainty $\Delta R_{\text{max}} = \phantom{ }$ \[ \begin{align*} \Delta R_{\text{max}} &= \left| \frac{\partial R}{\partial V} \Delta V \right| + \left| \frac{\partial R}{\partial I} \Delta I \right| \\ &= \left| \frac{1}{10} \cdot 5 \right| + \left| -\frac{100}{10^2} \cdot 0.5 \right| \\ &= 0.5 + 0.5 = 1 \text{ ohm} \end{align*} \]
  • RSS Uncertainty $\Delta R_{\text{RSS}} = \phantom{ }$ \[ \begin{align*} &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial V} \Delta V \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial I} \Delta I \right)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{\left(\frac{5}{10}\right)^2 + \left(-\frac{100 \cdot 0.5}{100^2}\right)^2} \\ &= \sqrt{0.25 + 0.25} = 0.5 \text{ ohms} \end{align*} \]

This slide illustrates the application of both maximum possible uncertainty and RSS methods to calculate the uncertainty in resistance, derived from Ohm's Law.

  • Maximum Possible Uncertainty: Indicates the highest possible error in \(R\), providing a conservative estimate for safety-critical applications.
  • RSS Uncertainty: Offers a more probable estimate of error, reflecting typical variations expected under normal measurement conditions.

Estimating Uncertainty: Gauge Fluid Density

Calculate the permissible uncertainty in the density of a gauge fluid (with a nominal density of $2500 \, \text{kg/m}^3 $) to maintain a pressure measurement accuracy of 0.1% for a maximum pressure of 10 kPa in a well manometer (with an uncertainty of $\frac{1}{10}\, \text{mm}$).

Uncertainty propagation: \[ \class{info}{\Delta P} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial \rho} \class{danger}{\Delta \rho} \right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial h} \class{warning}{\Delta h} \right)^2} \]

Given:

  • Formula for pressure: \( P = \class{pop}{\rho} \class{success}{g} \class{pink}{h} \)
  • Nominal fluid density \( \class{pop}{\rho = 2500} \, \text{kg/m}^3 \)
  • Gravitational constant \( \class{success}{g = 9.81} \, \text{m/s}^2 \)
  • Uncertainty in $ h $ ($\class{warning}{\Delta h}$): $\frac{1}{10}\, \text{mm} = \class{warning}{0.0001} \, \text{m} $
  • Maximum pressure uncertainty \( \class{info}{\Delta P} = 1\% \times (10000) = \class{info}{10} \, \text{Pa} \)
  • Derive \( \class{pink}{h} \) from \( P \): \[ \class{pink}{h} = \frac{P}{\class{pop}{\rho} \class{success}{g}} = \frac{10000}{\class{pop}{2500} \times \class{success}{9.81}} \approx \class{pink}{0.408} \, \text{m} \]

Substitution and Solution:

  • Partial for $\rho$: $ \frac{\partial P}{\partial \rho} = \class{success}{g}\class{pink}{h} \rightarrow 9.81 \times 0.408 = 4.00488 \, \text{m}^{2}\cdot\text{s}^{-2} $
  • Partial for $h$: $ \frac{\partial P}{\partial h} = \class{success}{g}\class{pop}{\rho} \rightarrow 9.81 \times 2500 = 24525 \, \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^{-2}\cdot\text{s}^{-2} $
  • Solve for \( \Delta \rho \): \[ \class{danger}{\Delta \rho} = \frac{\sqrt{\Delta P^2 - \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial h} \Delta h\right)^2}}{\frac{\partial P}{\partial \rho}} \] \[ = \frac{\sqrt{100 - \left(24525 \times \class{warning}{0.0001}\right)^2}}{4.00488} \] \[ \approx 2.42 \, \text{kg/m}^3 \]

The calculated $\Delta\rho = \pm 2.42 \, \text{kg/m}^3$ represents the maximum allowable uncertainty in the density of the gauge fluid to ensure pressure measurements remain within the desired accuracy of 0.1% of the maximum reading .

What is permissible uncertainty? Well, in the uncertainty propagation formula, it is the delta rho, or uncertainty of the fluid density.

This calculation highlights the sensitivity of pressure measurements to the accuracy of fluid density in manometric systems. In practical applications, ensuring the accuracy of density measurements is crucial, especially in systems where precise pressure measurements are critical.

Engineers and technicians must therefore carefully calibrate and verify the density of the gauge fluids used in precision instruments to adhere to stringent measurement standards. This example underscores the importance of thorough understanding and management of all potential sources of measurement uncertainty in engineering applications to achieve the desired precision and reliability in outcomes.

Sources Of Uncertainty

Random And Systemic Errors

Section Notes

Introduction to Sources of Measurement Uncertainty

Measurement uncertainty can arise from numerous sources during experimental testing and data analysis. Understanding these sources is crucial for accurate measurement and error management:

  • Systematic Errors: Errors that are not taken by chance but are consistent and predictable within the same measurement conditions.
    • Often due to measurement device calibration errors, environmental conditions, and methodological errors.
  • Random Errors: Errors that vary in an unpredictable manner over different measurements under the same conditions.
    • Typically arise from unpredictable fluctuations like sensor noise, operator variability, and inherent material inconsistencies.

Systemic: Independent of sample size.

Random: Depends on sample size.

Common Sources in Engineering Contexts

Identifying common sources of errors helps in optimizing measurement techniques and improving accuracy:

  • A/D Conversion Errors: Variability in analog-to-digital conversion can introduce errors in digital measurement systems.
  • Sensitivity and Linearity Errors: Deviations in sensor responses that can mislead measurement interpretations.
  • Environmental Factors: Temperature, humidity, and electromagnetic interference that can alter sensor outputs.

Discuss real-world implications and mitigation strategies for these common error sources, preparing students for practical measurement challenges in their engineering careers.

Understanding Random Uncertainties

Random uncertainties fluctuate unpredictably and depend on sample size. They are typically estimated using statistical methods:

  • Standard deviation is key for expressing random uncertainty.
  • Utilize t-distribution for calculating uncertainties with known degrees of freedom.
  • Simulation by Monte Carlo Resampling allows for empirical quantification of uncertainty and correction of inherent biases.

Understanding Random Uncertainty

Random uncertainty, denoted as \( \class{info strong}{P_x} \), varies unpredictably with each measurement and is quantified statistically. According to ASTM (1998), it's essential to evaluate this alongside systematic uncertainty \( \class{warning strong}{B_x} \) under the same confidence level:

  • ASTM Definition: Random uncertainty should be quantified at a confidence level that ensures the actual measurement error is within the estimated uncertainty limits, typically 95% confidence.
  • Mathematical Representation: The total uncertainty \( W_x \) in a measurement \( x \) is given by: \[ W_x = \left( \class{warning}{B_x}^2 + \class{info}{P_x}^2 \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \]

These calculations are essential for accurately accounting for all types of uncertainties, thereby enhancing the precision and dependability of measurement results.

This formula combines systematic (B_x) and random (P_x) uncertainties to calculate the overall uncertainty.

Application of Uncertainty Formulas

This understanding of uncertainty quantification is crucial for practical applications where precise measurements are necessary:

  • Implementing these methods in experimental setups helps ensure measurement errors can be predicted and minimized.
  • It allows engineers and scientists to design experiments and systems that operate within acceptable risk levels.

Calculations for Random Uncertainty

Mean Measurement Uncertainty:

When averaging multiple measurements, the uncertainty of the mean \( \bar{x} \), denoted as \( P_{\bar{x}} \), is reduced by the square root of the number of measurements \( n \):

\[ P_{\bar{x}} = \pm t \frac{S_x}{\sqrt{n}} \]

This formula applies when the measurements are of the same variable under identical conditions, improving precision by increasing the sample size.

Individual Measurement Uncertainty:

For a single measurement where no averaging is involved, the uncertainty \( P_x \) is estimated as:

$$P_x = t \cdot S_x$$

where \( t \) is the value from the t-distribution corresponding to the desired confidence level (typically 95%), and \( S_x \) is the standard deviation of the measurements.

Mean Measurement Uncertainty Across Multiple Sets:

When deriving the mean value \( \bar{x}_{\text{final}} \) from different batches or groups, each potentially having its own variance and mean, the uncertainty is calculated as:

$$P_{\bar{x}_{\text{final}}} = \frac{S_x}{\sqrt{M}}$$

This method is used when \( M \) represents the number of independent sets of measurements, not just the number of measurements within a single set.

Total Uncertainty:

The total uncertainty \( W_x \) for any measurement can be calculated with the Root Sum Square (RSS) method, which combines different sources of uncertainties:

For individual measurements:

$$W_x = \sqrt{B_x^2 + P_x^2}$$

For mean measurements:

$$W_{\class{danger}{\bar{x}}} = \sqrt{B_{x}^2 + P_{\class{danger}{\bar{x}}}^2}$$

In both formulas, \( B_x \) represents systematic uncertainties, while \( P_x \) and \( P_{\class{danger}{\bar{x}}} \) represent random uncertainties for individual and mean measurements, respectively. This distinction highlights how systematic and random uncertainties are managed differently depending on the aggregation of data.

Mean Measurement Uncertainty: Explains reduction in uncertainty for mean measurements, emphasizing precision increase with larger sample sizes.

Individual Measurement Uncertainty: Describes how uncertainty for a single measurement is calculated using the t-distribution, focusing on the impact of measurement variability.

Mean Measurement Uncertainty Across Multiple Sets: Discusses calculating uncertainty for averages across different groups, highlighting the method's applicability to diverse data sets.

Total Uncertainty Using RSS: Discusses the RSS method for combining uncertainties, detailing formulas for individual and aggregate data to highlight different management approaches based on data aggregation.

Uncertainty: Mass Measurements

In this example, we analyze the mass measurements taken by load cells in a chemical manufacturing process. The load cells measure the mass of a chemical mixture during a batch production.

Here are the key details:

  • Total measurements taken: 10
  • Average measured mass: 750 kg
  • Known standard deviation from historical data: 15 kg
  • Confidence level for uncertainty: 95%(t-value = 2.0)
    • For an unknown but large number of measurements for $S_x$, we use $t_{95\%,\nu=60} = 2.0$ as a common approximation.

Assumption: The load cells are precise, introducing no additional random uncertainty into the measurements.

Objective:

  1. Calculate the standard deviation and random uncertainty for each individual measurement.
  2. Determine the standard deviation and random uncertainty for the average of the ten measurements.

Uncertainty: Mass Measurements

a) Individual Measurement Uncertainty:

  • Standard deviation per measurement: 15 kg
  • Random uncertainty (95% confidence level): \[ P_x = t \cdot S_x = 2.0 \cdot 15 \, \text{kg} = 30 \, \text{kg} \]

b) Mean Measurement Uncertainty:

  • Standard deviation of the mean (n=10): \[ S_{\bar{x}} = \frac{S_x}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{15}{\sqrt{10}} \approx 4.74 \, \text{kg} \]
  • Random uncertainty of the mean (95% confidence level): \[ P_{\bar{x}} = t \cdot S_{\bar{x}} = 2.0 \cdot 4.74 \, \text{kg} \approx 9.48 \, \text{kg} \]

Elemental Uncertainty

Inherent And Unpredictable Variability

Section Notes

Introduction to Elemental Random Uncertainty

Understanding the sources and impacts of elemental random uncertainties is crucial in experimental methods. This section introduces key concepts and classifications according to ASME guidelines.

  • What is Elemental Random Uncertainty? It refers to the variability in measurement results that is inherent and unpredictable, affecting the accuracy of all experimental data.
  • Importance: Identifying and quantifying these uncertainties helps in improving the reliability and validity of experimental results.
  • Approach: We will discuss how to combine random uncertainties from different system components to provide a comprehensive error analysis.

Types of Elemental Errors

Elemental errors can be broadly categorized into several types, each affecting the accuracy and precision of measurements in unique ways:

  • Calibration Uncertainties: Errors stemming from calibration procedures, affecting how accurately instruments measure true values.
  • Data Acquisition Errors: Variability introduced during the collection of data, including A/D conversion errors and signal noise.
  • Data Reduction Errors: Approximations and assumptions made during data processing that can introduce discrepancies.
  • Methodological Uncertainties: Errors inherent in the measurement techniques and procedures used.

Note on t-value

If the sample size sizes are greater than 30 for all elemental random uncertainties combined in $S_x=\left[ \sum_{i=1}^mS_i^2\right] ^{\frac{1}{2}}$, then $t$ is only a function of the confidence level.

If any sample has $n < 30$, it is necessary to find a combined value for the degrees of freedom parameter, $\nu$:

ASME (1998) recommends the Welch-Satterthwaite formula:

$$V_x=\frac{\left[ \sum_{i=1}^mS_i^2\right] ^2}{\sum_{i=1}^m\left( \frac{S_i^4}{\nu_i}\right) }$$

where $\nu_i$ is the degrees of freedom of the individual elemental error, $m$ is the number of elemental random uncertainties combined in $S_x=\left[ \sum_{i=1}^mS_i^2\right] ^{\frac{1}{2}}$.

Combining Elemental Uncertainties

This section explains the process of integrating random uncertainties from various system components to estimate the total system uncertainty:

  • Root Sum Square (RSS) Method: A statistical method used to combine multiple independent uncertainties to form a comprehensive total uncertainty.
  • Application: Essential in complex measurement systems where multiple sources of error exist, RSS provides a way to quantify the combined effect on the system's accuracy.
  • Formula: For uncertainties \( \sigma_1, \sigma_2, ..., \sigma_n \), \[ \text{Total Uncertainty} = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \ldots + \sigma_n^2} \]

Understanding how to properly combine uncertainties ensures that the overall measurement system reliability is accurately assessed and reported.

Example 5: Temperature Measurement Uncertainty

In the control of a chemical process, temperature is measured by a sensor that, according to the manufacturer,has a calibration uncertainty of $\pm 0.5^\circ\text{C}$. In $20$ measurements in a test of the temperature sensor's repeatability, an average value of $150^\circ\text{C}$ with a standard deviation of $1.5^\circ\text{C}$ was determined. It is estimated that there can be a spatial variation of $\pm 2^\circ\text{C}$ and an installation effect of $\pm 1^\circ\text{C}$. In a separate test of the data-transmission system, it was determined that, for $10$ measurments the standard deviation was $0.5^\circ\text{C}$. The control program uses a linear relation between temperature and voltage, which can introduce an uncertainty of up to $\pm 1^\circ\text{C}$ in the applicable range.
