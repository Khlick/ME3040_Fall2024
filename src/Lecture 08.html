Choosing the right \(\alpha\) is crucial for accurate Q-Q plots:

Viewing Normality: QQ plot

  • The sample data are plotted against the standard normal (theoretical) quantiles.
  • The unity line represents perfect normality; sample points should cluster around this line.
  • Points on the unity line suggest that the sample distribution is similar to a normal distribution.
  • Deviation from the unity line implies potential skewness or kurtosis in the sample distribution.
  • The Quantile-Quantile Plot provides a visual comparison between empirical data quantiles and theoretical normal quantiles.

The QQ plot visualizes the sample quantiles against the theoretical quantiles from a standard normal distribution. A linear relationship suggests normality; deviations from this can inform about the distribution's skewness or tail behavior.

Interpreting QQ Plots

Deviations from the unity line have different implications:

  • Normal: Points closely follow the unity line, indicating a normal distribution.
  • Left Skew: Points curve below at the lower end and below the line at the upper end, reflecting a longer tail on the left and shorter on the right.
  • Right Skew: Points curve above the line at the lower end and above at the upper end, indicating a longer tail on the right and shorter on the left.

Interpreting QQ Plots

Deviations from the unity line have different implications:

  • Bimodal: Shows a distinctive "S" shaped curve or multiple clusters along the QQ line, indicating multiple peaks in the data distribution.
  • Fat Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, suggesting excess kurtosis and a higher likelihood of extreme values (i.e., longer tails at both ends).
  • Skinny Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, indicating a lower likelihood of extreme values (i.e., shorter tails on both sides).

Note that bimodal crosses in the middle and then also has skinny tails here. Where the skinny tails distribution doesn't cross so much (but might have multiple peaks randomly due to construction)

The standard error may be thought of as a 'typical' distance between the sample statistics and the population parameter.

To calculate standard error of the mean, we fist calculate the standard deviation of the sample.

Then we divide by $\sqrt{n}$ (where $n$ is our sample size.)

$$SE=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$

$SE$ estimates the spread of our sampling distribution!

Note: The variability of a sample statistic, called the standard error (SE), is distinct yet related to the standard deviation of sample values.

We will see this term standard a lot, note that we estimate.

It turns out, if our sample size (for one experiment) is large enough, we can estimate how far off we might be, or rather how UNCERTAIN we are about our sample statistic (mean) representing the population parameter (mu).

With this information, we can put a number to how uncertain we are.

Confidence Intervals

The sampling distribution of a statistic, normally distributed around the population parameter, allows us to quantify the likelihood of a sample statistic's proximity to the true population parameter.

For example, 95% of our statistics fall within $\pm 1.96\times SE$ of the parameter, indicating a 95% probability that the true parameter lies within this range from any observed statistic.

This range is termed a confidence interval.

Confidence intervals quantify the uncertainty associated with estimating the parameter.

Uncertainty is expressed in "confidence intervals". It acknowledges that the exact value of the parameter (such as the mean or proportion) is unknown and might not be precisely determined from a sample.

While we typically will use 95% for our confidence intervals, it is common to see other intervals, such as 90 or 99%.

Where does this 1.96 come from?

Standardized Statistic

The z-score, also known as a standard score, measures the number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean.

Z-scores are utilized to identify and describe the position of a data point within a distribution, assess probabilities, and detect outliers.

Normal Distribution

The Z Score effectively standardizes observations, allowing us to make comparisons across different distributions.

$$z(X) = \frac{x_i - \bar{X}}{s}$$

Where $x_i$ is the data point, $\bar{X}$ is the mean of the sample, and $s$ is the standard deviation.

Calculating Z-Score for 95% Confidence

Normal Distribution

To understand the probability associated with a specific z-score, we calculate $\Phi(z)$, representing the cumulative probability up to that z-score in a standard normal distribution.

$$\Phi(z) = F(z \mid 0, 1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{z} e^{-\frac{t^2}{2}} \, dt, \quad \text{for } z \in \mathbb{R}. $$

Here, $\Phi(z)$ is the cumulative probability from the left tail of the distribution up to a z-score of $z$, $t$ is a variable of integration, and $e$ is the base of the natural logarithm.

The area under the curve from $-\infty$ to $z$ gives us the cumulative probability, the compliment is $1 - \Phi(z)$.

For a 95% confidence interval in a standard normal distribution, we use the z-score value of $\pm 1.96$, which corresponds to the critical value that marks the boundaries of the central 95% of the distribution.

This implies that approximately 95% of all statistics fall within $\pm 1.96$ standard deviations from the mean.

This is the integral of the normal distribution's probability density function (PDF)

Here 'all statistics' means the distribution of the sampling statistic if the number of replicates goes to infinity.

Discrete Example: Coin Flips

Estimate the 95% confidence interval for 19 Heads in 28 coin flips.

$$\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n} = \frac{19}{28}\,\&\, SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1 - \hat{p})}{n}}$$

Where $\hat{p}$ is the sample expected proportion, $x$ is the number of successes (heads), and $n$ is the total number of trials (coin flips).

For a 95% confidence interval, we use the z-score corresponding to 95% confidence, which is approximately 1.96:

$$\begin{aligned} CI &= \hat{p} \pm z \cdot SE \\ &= \frac{19}{28} \pm 1.96 \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\frac{19}{28}(1 - \frac{19}{28})}{28}} \\ &\approx \frac{19}{28} \pm 0.181 \\ &\approx 19 \pm \sim 5\, \text{Heads} \end{aligned}$$

This means we are 95% confident that the true proportion of heads in the population from which this sample was drawn falls within this interval.

Chance-model: Binomial Distribution$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$ for $k={1,\ldots,28}$:

If we assume $E[H]=\hat{\pi}=0.5$, then $n\hat{\pi}=14$ is our expected count if the chance-model is true, does our 95% confidence interval include the by-chance value?

Thinking about values that lie within intervals, this is the statistical evidence. Anyone remember: "If the glove don't fit, you must acquit" OJSimpson's lawyers.

  • The sample proportion of heads is: \( \hat{p} = \frac{19}{28} \approx 0.679 \).
  • Calculate the Standard Error (SE): \( SE = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p} (1 - \hat{p})}{n}} = \sqrt{\frac{\frac{19}{28} \left(1 - \frac{19}{28}\right)}{28}} \approx 0.092 \).
  • Calculate the 95% confidence interval for the proportion: \( CI = \hat{p} \pm z \cdot SE = 0.679 \pm 1.96 \cdot 0.092 \approx 0.679 \pm 0.181 \).
  • Convert the proportion interval to the count of heads:
    • Lower bound: \( (0.679 - 0.181) \times 28 \approx 0.498 \times 28 \approx 14 \).
    • Upper bound: \( (0.679 + 0.181) \times 28 \approx 0.860 \times 28 \approx 24 \).
  • Thus, the confidence interval for the number of heads is approximately: \( 19 \pm 5 \text{ heads} \) (from 14 to 24 heads).

Introduction to Null Hypothesis Testing

Null Hypothesis Testing is a statistical method that enables decision making through data analysis. It begins with the assumption that no effect, difference, or relationship exists in a population.

The Null Hypothesis ($H_0$) proposes that there is no significant difference or effect, serving as a default position that a statement or effect is not present.

The Alternative Hypothesis ($H_a$ or $H_1$) suggests that there is a significant difference or effect, contradicting the null hypothesis.

The testing procedure involves:

  • Defining $H_0$ and $H_a$.
  • Choosing a significance level ($\alpha$), commonly 0.05.
  • Calculating a test statistic based on sample data.
  • Comparing the statistic to a critical value or using a p-value to determine if $H_0$ can be rejected.

The purpose of null hypothesis testing is to provide a systematic way to test claims or hypotheses about a population parameter, facilitating evidence-based decision making.

Let's add a new layer of terminology: Now we have a new, sort of, hypothesis. We had scientific and statistical, and now let's add a layer onto the statistical hypothesis:

Hypothesis testing in statistics refers to the practice of determining a p-value for an experiment or test. This p-value represents the probability of observing a statistic at least as extreme as the one we got.

Null Hypothesis Testing

The Null and Alternative Hypotheses add specificity to the general idea of a statistical hypothesis.

While a general statistical hypothesis makes a statement about the data, the null and alternative hypotheses refine this into testable statements that can guide statistical testing:

Null Hypothesis $H_0$

The null hypothesis is a default position that states there is no effect, no difference, or no association in the data concerning what we are testing. It represents the a baseline belief that any observed effect in the data is due to random chance rather than a true underlying effect. In many cases, the null hypothesis is what we aim to test against.

Example: If we are testing whether a new drug lowers blood pressure more than a placebo, the null hypothesis might be that there is no difference in blood pressure reduction between the drug and the placebo.

Alternative Hypothesis $H_1$

The alternative hypothesis is the complement to the null hypothesis and represents what we are trying to provide evidence for. It posits that there is a real effect, difference, or association in the data. The alternative hypothesis is what researchers typically hope to support through their data analysis.

Example: In the same drug study, the alternative hypothesis would be that the new drug lowers blood pressure more than the placebo.

General: Is there a difference in blood pressure between the drug and placebo groups

Null: All possible differences between drug and placebo groups are distributed around 0, with parameters that depend on the distribution type.

Alternate: All possible differences between drug and placebo groups are distributed around some true effect-size that are extreme enough to consider plausibly non-zero, or non-null.

What is a p-value?

A p-value is the probability of obtaining a result at least as extreme as the one observed, assuming that the null hypothesis is true.

A small p-value casts doubt on the null hypothesis/model used to perform the calculation (in the blood pressure student, $H_0$ was changes in BP were randomly different from 0).

Z - test: Known Population Variance

When the population parameters ($\mu,\, \sigma$) are known, the z-test is used to determine if there's a significant difference between the sample mean ($\bar{x}$) and the population mean ($\mu$).

  • $\bar{X}$ is the sample mean.
  • $\mu$ is the population mean.
  • $\sigma$ is the population standard deviation.
  • $\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}$ is the standard error ($\text{SE}_{\mu}$) of the population
  • $N$ is the sample size.
$$Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}}}$$

If the population variance is not given, we can estimate our Z and SE from the sample standard deviation: $Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}}$

We compare $Z$ to the critical range of the population's sampling distribution to decide whether to reject the null hypothesis. Typically, this is the set at a point $1-\alpha$.

Let's take a second and think about what this implies or assumes about the underlying distribution.

Z-Test is the test to determine if two means are different, assuming they came from the same underlying distribution. Like comparing Z-Scores

This says, if both our sample and the population have the same underlying sampling distribution of the mean, then Z is the standardized difference between our sample mean and the population parameter.

This allows us to ask the question, what is the probability of finding our sample mean from the population's sampling distribution.

Alpha determines the cutoff Z value, beyond which we call more extreme. If our Z value is that extreme, we say it is "Statistically significantly different" from the population mean.

A typical value for alpha is 0.05, which corresponds to the 95% CI of N(0,1).
