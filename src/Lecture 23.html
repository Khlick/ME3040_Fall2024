The theoretical approach to determine the $\chi^2$ distribution requires the use of the correct number of degrees of freedom of the contingency table you are studying. Remember that you would also need a count>10 for all the cells as validity condition of the theoretical approach.

$\chi^2$: Expected Counts

Recall that $\chi^2$ is the relative squared distance between the observed and expected counts in a contingency table.

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i}\sum_{j}\frac{(O_{ij}-E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$$

For a given contingency table:

YesNo
Male7040
Female5055

We can compute the expected count for each cell using the $n_{group}\cdot p(\text{outcome})$ approach:

Yes No
Male $E(Y|M) = n_M \cdot p(Y) = 110 \times \frac{120}{215} = 61.4$ $E(N|M) = n_M \cdot p(N) = 110 \times \frac{95}{215} = 48.6$
Female $E(Y|F) = n_F \cdot p(Y) = 105 \times \frac{120}{215} = 58.6$ $E(N|F) = n_F \cdot p(N) = 105 \times \frac{95}{215} = 46.4$

Which can be neatly performed using the outer product, $\phantom{} \otimes\phantom{}$, such that $E(\, \text{Condition}|\, \text{Group}) = \overrightarrow{n_G}\otimes \overrightarrow{p_C}$, which becomes:

$$\begin{bmatrix} E(Y|M) & E(N|M) \\ E(Y|F) & E(N|F) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}n_M \\ n_F\end{bmatrix} \bigotimes\, ( p(Y)\, p(N)) $$

$\chi^2$: Calculation

Given $\, O=\begin{pmatrix}70 & 40\\ 50 & 55\end{pmatrix}$ and $\, E=\begin{pmatrix}61 & 49 \\59 & 46\end{pmatrix}$

Then,

$$ \chi^{2}=\sum \frac{ \left[ \begin{pmatrix} \class{info}{70} & \class{info}{40}\\ \class{orange}{50} & \class{orange}{55} \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 61 & 49\\ 59 & 46 \end{pmatrix}\right]^{2} }{ \begin{pmatrix} 61 & 49\\ 59 & 46 \end{pmatrix} } $$

Conceptually, we can split each component into its own $\chi^2$ calculation, such that the total $\chi^2$ is the sum of the constituents: $\chi^2=\phantom{ }$ ${\class{info}\chi^{\class{info}2}_{\class{info}{Male}}}$ $\phantom{ }+{\class{orange}\chi^{\class{orange}2}_{\class{orange}{Female}}}$.

$$\begin{aligned} \chi^2 &= \left[ \frac{(\class{info}{70}-61)^{2}}{61} + \frac{(\class{info}{40}-49)^{2}}{49} \right]_{\class{info}{Male}} \\ &+ \left[ \frac{(\class{orange}{50}-59)^{2}}{59} + \frac{(\class{orange}{55}-46)^{2}}{46} \right]_{\class{orange}{Female}} \\ \therefore \chi^2 &= \left[ \frac{81}{61} + \frac{81}{49} \right]_{\class{info}{Male}} + \left[ \frac{81}{59} + \frac{81}{46} \right]_{\class{orange}{Female}} \\ \phantom{ } &= \class{info}{2.98} + \class{orange}{3.13} \\ \phantom{ } &= 6.11 \end{aligned} $$

We can plot the Yes-No pairs for the observed, expected counts. The resulting squares are unscaled square areas.

What If Counts Are Very Few?

$\chi^2$ requires the following conditions:

  • When any elements are zero, $\chi^2$ is not possible (think about the process).
  • When any value is $\phantom{}< 10$, the Gaussian assumptions in $\chi^2$ fails.
  • $\chi^2$ is generally defined with free marginals.

For example, let's look at this small table:

Diabetes No Diabetes
Male 5 1
Female 1 4

If we demand that row and table totals (marginals) remain constant, then there are a finite number of possible tables. In this case, there are 6:

$${\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}6&0\\0&5\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}5&1\\1&4\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}4&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}3&3\\3&2\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}2&4\\4&1\end{pmatrix}},\quad {\begin{pmatrix}1&5\\5&0\end{pmatrix}}.} $$

Thus, there are only 6 possible $\chi^2$ values: $0.11,0.78,2.4,4.41,7.64, 11.00$.

Which makes it hard to interpret the $\chi^2$ distribution!

Free Marginals in Chi-Squared Analysis

In chi-squared analysis, free marginals refer to contingency tables where row and column totals are not pre-determined but observed from the data. This setup is common in observational studies where the researcher does not control how data falls into categories.

The chi-squared test assesses whether there's an association between row and column variables, assuming that the observed frequencies in the table arise purely due to the underlying relationship between these variables.

Expected frequencies are calculated as:

$$E_{ij} = \frac{(Row\ Total_i \times Column\ Total_j)}{Grand\ Total}$$

Here, each expected count \(E_{ij}\) is based on the proportionate contribution of the corresponding row and column totals to the grand total, reflecting the independence assumption of the test.

Background of Fisher's Exact Test

Fisher's Exact Test was developed from an intriguing real-life scenario involving Muriel Bristol, a scientist at the Rothamsted Experiment Station. Bristol claimed she could discern whether milk or tea was poured first into a cup.

To test her assertion, Ronald Fisher devised a simple experiment: eight cups of tea were prepared, four with milk poured first and four with tea poured first. The marginals-four cups for each method-were fixed.

Bristol was then asked to taste each cup and identify the preparation method for four of them. This setup created an ideal scenario for using Fisher's Exact Test to determine if Bristol's claims were statistically significant, given the fixed margins and small sample size.

Stress the importance of the context in which Fisher's Exact Test was developed. Discuss its relevance to small sample sizes and fixed marginals. Mention that this anecdote illustrates how statistical methods can be applied to solve practical, everyday problems.

Fisher's Exact Test

By fixing the marginals, Fisher could then determine the probability of getting the exact table observed.

He did this by combinatorics:

$${\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}a_1&a_2\\b_1&b_2\end{pmatrix}}}$$

$${\displaystyle p_{\text{table}} = \frac{ \binom{a_1+a_2}{a_1} \binom{b_1+b_2}{b_1} }{ \binom{a_1+a_2+b_1+b_2}{a_1+b_1} } }$$

This gets pretty messy when expanded to factorial form:

$${\displaystyle \frac{ (a_1+a_2)!\, (b_1+b_2)!\, (a_1+b_1)!\, (a_2+b_2)!\, }{ a_1!\,a_2!\,b_1!\,b_2!\,(a_1+a_2+b_1+b_2)! }}$$

$${\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&1\\2&2\\3&6\\4&24\\5&120\\6&720\\7&5,040\\8&40,320\\9&362,880\\10&3,628,800\\11&39,916,800\\12&479,001,600\\13&6,227,020,800\\14&87,178,291,200\end{pmatrix}}}$$

Bristol was presented with 8 cups of tea, 4 with milk poured first and 4 with tea poured first, and asked to identify the preparation method for each cup.

Milk 1st (Actual) Tea 1st (Actual)

${\displaystyle p=\frac{(4+0)!\,(0+4)!\,(4+0)!\,(0+4)!}{4!\,0!\,0!\,4!\,(4+0+0+4)!}}= 0.0142857$

And all the probabilities (sorted):

$\{ 0.0142857,\quad 0.0857143,\quad 0.2285714,\quad 0.2285714\}$

Since she correctly identified all 8 cups, she had a probability of $0.0142857$ of getting it right by chance.

Is this proof of her ability?

A p-value of 0.014 is strong evidence against the null hypothesis that she was guessing, but Fisher emphasized this didn't prove her ability but demonstrated the framework for statistical inference.

Again, no standard approach to the post-hoc, but it wasn't needed here.

Understanding Error

Power And Uncertainty

Types Of Inference

Hypothesis testing ($\alpha$ level)

Hypothesis tests and p-values are most useful when you want to test hypotheses about population parameters.

p-values quantify the strength of evidence against the null hypothesis.

$\alpha$ confidence intervals

Confidence intervals are most useful when you want to estimate population parameters.

Confidence intervals give you a range of plausible values for your parameter.

Note: Usually $\alpha$=0.05.

Sir Ronald Fisher introduced the concept of the 0.05 level as a cut-off point for statistical significance, but he did not intend for it to become as definitive and universally applied as it has become in contemporary research. Fisher suggested that a p-value of 0.05 was a convenient threshold to consider a result sufficiently rare under the null hypothesis, thereby justifying its rejection.

The History of the Alpha Level in Hypothesis Testing

The alpha level of 0.05, a standard threshold for statistical significance, was popularized by Ronald A. Fisher in the 1920s. Originally suggested as a convenient cut-off for rejecting the null hypothesis, it implied a result would only occur by chance about 1 in 20 times under the null hypothesis.

It is convenient to take this point as a limit in judging whether a deviation is to be considered significant or not.

-Sir Ronald Fisher

Despite its arbitrary nature, the 0.05 level became widely adopted due to its balance between Type I and Type II errors. Over time, it has become the conventional standard across various scientific disciplines.

Recent debates challenge this standard, suggesting adjustments or alternative approaches like confidence intervals or Bayesian statistics to better reflect the complexity of scientific evidence.

What is the probability of making a Type I or Type II error?

Probability of Type I Errors

If the null hypothesis is true:

  • 5% of statistics will be in the most extreme 5%
  • 5% of statistics will give p-values less than 0.05
  • 5% of statistics will lead to rejecting $H_0$ at $\alpha=0.05$

If $\alpha=0.05$, there is a 5% chance of a Type I error

If the null hypothesis is true:

  • 1% of statistics will be in the most extreme 1%
  • 1% of statistics will give p-values less than 0.01
  • 1% of statistics will lead to rejecting $H_0$ at $\alpha=0.01$

If $\alpha=0.01$, there is a 1% chance of a Type I error

For each test, the probability of making a Type I error is $\alpha$.

Probability of Type II Errors

How can we reduce the probability of making a Type II Error

That is, how could we increase the probability of rejecting the null when the null hypothesis is rejectable, i.e., false?

1 Increase the sample size

Larger sample sizes decrease the variability of the sampling distribution.

  • With small sample sizes, even large differences or effects may not be statistically significant, and Type II errors are common.
  • But with large sample sizes, even a very small difference or effect can be statistically significant

Increased $n$ reduces chances of Type II errors increases statistical power.

That is, how could we increase the probability of rejecting the null when the null hypothesis is rejectable, i.e., false?

1 Increase the sample size

2 Increase the significance level

Increase $\alpha$ to reduce the chance of Type II error.

Decrease $\alpha$ to reduce the chance of Type I error.

Significance Level and Errors

While we wish to avoid both types of errors, in practice we must accept some trade-off between them:

If we set $\alpha$ too low:

  • It becomes more difficult to reject $H_0$ (conservative).
  • This decreases the probability of a Type I error (false positive).
  • But increases the probability of a Type II error (false negative).

If we set $\alpha$ too high:

  • It becomes easier to reject $H_0$ (liberal).
  • This decreases the probability of a Type II error (false negative).
  • But increases the probability of a Type I error (false positive).

Understanding The Significance Level

The significance level $\alpha$, represents the tolerable probability of making a Type I error.

If the consequences of a Type I error are severe (for example, approving a new drug that is potentially dangerous) we might use a very small $\alpha$ (perhaps even $\alpha=0.005$).

If the consequences of a Type II error are severe (for example, failing to diagnose a treatable disease), we would want to make it easier to reject $\mathrm{H}_0$, so we might use a relatively large $\alpha$.

Key idea

Remember that there is always a trade-off between the two types of errors, so people usually use the common significance levels of 5%, 10% or 1% (but where the perfect balance lies is a complicated question and the increasing subject of debate).
